Only in Bulgaria — A Political Party Game

For 500 years, Bulgaria lived under Ottoman rule. In 1878, it took a Russian army to break it free. The Russo-Turkish War ended five centuries of occupation. Slavic brotherhood, they called it. The debt has never been forgotten.

The 20th century was turbulent. Bulgaria lost territory after both world wars — backing the wrong side each time, though it famously refused to deport its Jewish population despite being an Axis ally in WWII. Its cities were bombed by the British. Then came communism. Whatever else it was, it brought a kind of stability. Bulgaria was so aligned with Moscow it applied multiple times to become the 16th Soviet republic. Moscow declined. There were discussions about joining Yugoslavia too.

The transition after 1989 was a disaster. State enterprises were sold for nearly nothing. Corruption spread through every institution. In 1996–97, the currency collapsed, inflation hit 1,000%, and people lined up to buy bread.

GDP per capita (USD) — Bulgaria 1990–2024

Then came Boyko Borisov. Former fire chief. Karate black belt. Bodyguard to Tsar Simeon II. He dominated Bulgarian politics for over a decade — Prime Minister three separate times. Always the largest party. Always surviving.

Until 2020, when a photo appeared: a man who looked exactly like the serving Prime Minister asleep in bed. A loaded Glock on the nightstand. Stacks of €500 notes. And what appeared to be gold bars.

The protests that followed changed everything. Or tried to.

Since 2021, Bulgaria has held 9 parliamentary elections.
Voter turnout: below 40%.
The voting booth became the national sport — and nobody wants to play.
